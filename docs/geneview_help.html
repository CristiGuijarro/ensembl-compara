• Orthologue Prediction - a link to the closest putative orthologue genes in other species. Links are given between human, mouse and rat with all other species including chimp, chicken, zebrafish, Takifugu rubripes, Anopheles gambiae, Drosophila melanogaster and Caenorhabditis elegans). A link at the bottom of this section leads to a corresponding Ensembl 'MultiContigView' page, which allows display of both genome sequence and annotation information for several species simultaneously. By default, all species with orthologues defined in this section are selected. Further species could be added or others dropped directly from 'MultiContigView' pages.

    The homologous genes represent the best reciprocal BLAST hits for the two species (Best Reciprocal Hit: BRH), with additional pairs obtained by a combination of BLAST and location information (Reciprocal Hit based on Synteny: RHS) for more closely related species. These homologues may therefore potentially represent orthologues.

    The chimp/human homologues are obtained from whole genome BLASTz alignments between human and chimp rather than by reciprocal BLASTP analysis and are designated DWGA (Derived from Whole Genome Alignments). This was also the method used to predict the chimp genes, by mapping the human genes onto the chimp chromosomes. See Description on the relevant Ensembl Gene Report.

    Clicking on the putative orthologue gene will take you to a 'GeneView' display of that gene within the web site for the other species. See also the 'SyntenyView' help page. Where no homology matches are shown, you may wish to explore the Ensembl protein family (see below).

    dN (number of non-synonymous substitutions / number of non-synonymous sites) and dS (number of synonymous substitutions / number of synonymous sites) values were generated using the codeml program included in the PAML package (1). With the parameters we have used, codeml performs pair wise Maximum Likelihood calculations of dN and dS for each set of orthologues. We have used the F3x4 codon evolution model (2). This takes into account both the bias deriving from the different probabilities of transition (T<->C and A<->G) versus transversion (T/C<->A/G) mutations, and the bias due to different nucleotide frequencies at the three codon positions.

    dN and dS values are only provided for orthologues from some species pairs, i.e. human/mouse, human/rat, and mouse/rat. Orthologues for other species pairs are too divergent for dS to be an accurate measure. Most synonymous sites will have be subjected to more than one mutation and ancestral changes cannot be reliably inferred from extant sequences, (i.e. dS is saturated).

    Orthologue predictions for human/mouse, human/rat and mouse/rat may not be perfect. Incorrect assignments will manifest anomalously high dS values. We have, therefore, applied a cut-off of twice the median value of all dS for each species pair as the criterion for displaying the dN/dS ratio. Predicted orthology relationships with dS above this threshold are likely to be errors.

    The following dS threshold values have been used: 

                       dS threshold

   human/mouse        1.26978
   human/rat          1.28408
   mouse/rat          0.40745

    Therefore, dN/dS ratios for human/mouse orthologues are only displayed when dS <= 1.26978, otherwise "--".

    NB: The dN, dS values for elegans/briggsae were calculated but dS values were considered as saturated (median = 2.26584, >> 1). After applying the dS threshold (4.53168), the median and average on the remaining set were 1.93962 and 2.062 respectively, and comparable with the data that was published in the Caenorhabditis briggsae genome paper (3), average 1.78 (no median was provided in the paper).

  • Paralogue Prediction - a link to putative paralogues.
    The identification of paralogues (recently duplicated human genes) has been conducted using a simple approach and it is only available for human at the moment.
    A raw set of gene homologies was derived from an all-versus-all BLAST approach against a dataset of Ensembl predicted genes including mouse and rat genes for out-groups. The resulting pairs were then filtered and ranked according to genetic distance and gene coverage. Groups of duplicated human genes were determined by clustering genes that shared common reciprocal matches. Hence, groups of recently duplicated genes identified in this manner have phylogenetic meaning and can be formally defined as being paralogous human genes that have arisen since the human-rodent divergence.
